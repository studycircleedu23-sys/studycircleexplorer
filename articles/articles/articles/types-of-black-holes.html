How Many Types of Black Holes Exist?

Astronomers classify black holes into several categories based on their mass and how they formed. The four main types are stellar black holes, intermediate-mass black holes, supermassive black holes, and the theoretical primordial black holes.

Each type plays a unique role in the evolution of stars, galaxies, and the universe itself.

⭐ Quick Fact

Although all black holes have extremely strong gravity, they can differ enormously in size—from a few times the Sun's mass to billions of solar masses.

1. Stellar Black Holes

Stellar black holes form when massive stars reach the end of their lives and collapse under their own gravity. They typically contain between 5 and 100 times the mass of the Sun.

2. Intermediate-Mass Black Holes

Intermediate black holes are believed to have masses ranging from hundreds to hundreds of thousands of solar masses. They are rare and remain one of astronomy's biggest mysteries.

3. Supermassive Black Holes

Supermassive black holes are the giants of the universe. They contain millions to billions of times the mass of the Sun and are found at the centers of most galaxies, including the Milky Way. Sagittarius A* is our galaxy's central supermassive black hole.

4. Primordial Black Holes

Primordial black holes are hypothetical objects that may have formed shortly after the Big Bang. Unlike other black holes, they would not originate from collapsing stars. Scientists continue searching for evidence of their existence.

Amazing Facts

  • ⭐ The Milky Way contains the supermassive black hole Sagittarius A*.
  • ⭐ TON 618 is one of the largest known black holes.
  • ⭐ Intermediate black holes are extremely difficult to detect.
  • ⭐ Primordial black holes remain hypothetical.
  • ⭐ Every major galaxy studied so far appears to host a supermassive black hole.

Quick Summary

Black holes come in different sizes and masses. Stellar black holes form from dying stars, intermediate black holes are rare, supermassive black holes dominate galaxy centers, and primordial black holes remain theoretical. Studying these different types helps scientists understand the evolution of stars, galaxies, and the universe itself.
