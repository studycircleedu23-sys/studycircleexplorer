🕳️ Boötes Void

Journey into one of the largest and emptiest known regions of the observable universe—a place where galaxies are astonishingly rare.

17 July 2026 15 min read Study Circle Explorer

What Is the Boötes Void?

The Boötes Void, often called the Great Nothing, is one of the largest known cosmic voids in the observable universe. Unlike galaxy clusters that are packed with galaxies, the Boötes Void is an enormous region containing remarkably few galaxies compared to its immense size.

It was discovered in 1981 by astronomer Robert Kirshner and his team during a survey of galaxy distributions. The discovery surprised scientists because such a vast empty region was not expected.

🌌 Did You Know?

If the Milky Way were placed inside the Boötes Void, our nearest neighboring galaxy might be hundreds of millions of light-years away instead of just 2.5 million light-years like Andromeda.

How Big Is the Boötes Void?

The Boötes Void has an estimated diameter of about 330 million light-years. Despite this enormous volume, only around 60 galaxies have been identified within it—far fewer than astronomers would expect in a region of this size.

Why Is It So Empty?

Astronomers believe the Boötes Void formed naturally during the evolution of the universe. As gravity pulled matter into dense filaments and galaxy clusters, some regions lost much of their matter, creating vast cosmic voids. Over billions of years these empty regions expanded while galaxies became concentrated within the surrounding Cosmic Web.

What Exists Inside the Void?

Although called a "void," the Boötes Void is not completely empty. It contains a small number of isolated galaxies, extremely thin clouds of gas, dark matter, and radiation. However, compared with the average density of the universe, it is extraordinarily empty.

How Does the Boötes Void Fit into the Cosmic Web?

The Boötes Void is surrounded by enormous filaments of galaxies that make up the Cosmic Web. These dense filaments contain galaxy groups, clusters, and superclusters, while the void itself represents one of the sparsest regions between those structures. Together, filaments and voids create the universe's sponge-like large-scale structure.

How Do Astronomers Study the Boötes Void?

Astronomers study the Boötes Void using powerful optical telescopes, radio observatories, and large-scale galaxy surveys such as the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS). By mapping the positions of millions of galaxies, scientists can identify enormous empty regions like the Boötes Void and understand how matter is distributed throughout the universe.

Dark Matter Inside the Void

Although the Boötes Void contains very few galaxies, it is not completely devoid of matter. Scientists believe that small amounts of dark matter, diffuse gas, and isolated galaxies still exist within the void. However, the density of both ordinary matter and dark matter is much lower than in galaxy clusters or filaments.

Could a Void Become Completely Empty?

Probably not. Even the emptiest known regions of space contain tiny amounts of matter, radiation, dark matter, and quantum particles. As the universe continues to expand, cosmic voids are expected to grow larger and become even less dense, but they are unlikely to become perfectly empty.

Why Are Cosmic Voids Important?

Cosmic voids provide valuable clues about the evolution of the universe. By comparing the distribution of galaxies inside dense clusters with the sparse populations inside voids, astronomers can test theories of gravity, dark matter, dark energy, and the growth of the Cosmic Web.

Why Is the Boötes Void Important?

The Boötes Void demonstrates that the universe is not uniformly filled with galaxies. Instead, galaxies are concentrated within enormous filaments and clusters, leaving vast empty spaces between them. Studying these voids helps astronomers understand how gravity shaped the large-scale structure of the universe over the past 13.8 billion years.
