🌌 Galaxy Groups

Discover the small families of galaxies that make up the building blocks of the universe's largest cosmic structures.

17 July 2026 14 min read Study Circle Explorer

What Are Galaxy Groups?

A Galaxy Group is a collection of galaxies held together by their mutual gravitational attraction. These systems are smaller than galaxy clusters and typically contain a few large galaxies along with dozens of smaller dwarf galaxies. In fact, most galaxies in the universe belong to galaxy groups rather than massive clusters.

Galaxy groups are the fundamental building blocks of the universe's large-scale structure. Over billions of years, many of these groups merge together to form the enormous galaxy clusters and superclusters that populate the cosmos.

🌌 Did You Know?

Our own Milky Way is part of the Local Group, a galaxy group that contains more than 100 known galaxies, including the Andromeda Galaxy and the Triangulum Galaxy.

How Big Are Galaxy Groups?

Galaxy groups usually span between 1 and 10 million light-years. Although much smaller than galaxy clusters, they still contain enormous amounts of matter, most of which is invisible dark matter surrounding the member galaxies.

How Many Galaxies Can a Group Contain?

Most galaxy groups contain between 3 and 50 major galaxies, along with many dwarf galaxies. Some rich groups, including the Local Group, have over 100 known members when dwarf galaxies are included.

Dark Matter in Galaxy Groups

Just like galaxy clusters, galaxy groups are dominated by dark matter. The visible galaxies account for only a small fraction of the group's total mass. The invisible dark matter halo provides the gravity needed to keep the galaxies bound together despite the enormous distances separating them.

🚀 Quick Galaxy Group Facts

  • ✔ Smaller than galaxy clusters.
  • ✔ Usually contain a few large galaxies and many dwarf galaxies.
  • ✔ Span approximately 1–10 million light-years.
  • ✔ Most of their mass is dark matter.
  • ✔ The Local Group is our home galaxy group.
  • ✔ Galaxy groups are the building blocks of galaxy clusters.

Galaxy Groups vs. Galaxy Clusters

Although both are gravitationally bound systems, galaxy groups are much smaller than galaxy clusters. Groups typically contain only a few large galaxies, while clusters can contain hundreds or even thousands. Galaxy clusters are also significantly more massive and possess much hotter gas that emits powerful X-rays.

Why Are Galaxy Groups Important?

Galaxy groups provide ideal environments for galaxies to interact. Their relatively low speeds allow galaxies to pass close to one another, exchange gas, trigger bursts of star formation, and eventually merge. These interactions play a key role in shaping the evolution of galaxies throughout cosmic history.

How Do Galaxy Groups Form?

Galaxy groups form as gravity gradually pulls nearby galaxies together over billions of years. Dark matter provides the invisible gravitational framework that binds these galaxies into a single system. Over time, smaller groups may merge with one another to form much larger structures known as galaxy clusters.

The Local Group

Our own galaxy, the Milky Way, belongs to a galaxy group called the Local Group. This group contains more than 100 known galaxies, including the Milky Way, the Andromeda Galaxy (M31), the Triangulum Galaxy (M33), and dozens of smaller dwarf galaxies. The Local Group spans roughly 10 million light-years across.

Galaxy Interactions

Galaxies within a group constantly influence one another through gravity. These interactions can distort galaxy shapes, trigger bursts of star formation, exchange gas, and sometimes result in galaxy mergers. Such interactions play a major role in the long-term evolution of galaxies.

Will the Milky Way Merge with Andromeda?

Yes. Astronomers predict that the Milky Way and the Andromeda Galaxy will collide and merge in approximately 4–5 billion years. Although individual stars are unlikely to collide because of the vast distances between them, gravity will eventually reshape both galaxies into a single large elliptical galaxy.

🚀 Amazing Galaxy Group Facts

  • ✔ Most galaxies in the universe belong to galaxy groups rather than massive clusters.
  • ✔ The Local Group contains more than 100 known galaxies.
  • ✔ Dark matter accounts for most of a galaxy group's total mass.
  • ✔ Galaxy groups are the building blocks of larger galaxy clusters.
  • ✔ The Milky Way and Andromeda dominate the Local Group.
  • ✔ Galaxy mergers are a natural part of galaxy group evolution.

Why Are Galaxy Groups Important?

Galaxy groups help astronomers understand how galaxies evolve through gravitational interactions. Since most galaxies exist in groups, studying these systems provides valuable insights into star formation, galaxy mergers, dark matter, and the growth of the universe's large-scale structure.

🌌 Quick Summary

• Galaxy groups contain a few to several dozen large galaxies plus many dwarf galaxies.
• They are held together primarily by gravity and dark matter.
• The Local Group is our home galaxy group.
• Galaxy interactions and mergers shape galaxy evolution.
• Galaxy groups eventually combine to form massive galaxy clusters.
