🌌 Laniakea Supercluster

Welcome to our true cosmic address—a gigantic network of galaxies stretching across more than half a billion light-years.

17 July 2026 13 min read Study Circle Explorer

What is the Laniakea Supercluster?

The Laniakea Supercluster is the enormous cosmic structure that contains our Milky Way, the Local Group, the Virgo Supercluster, and approximately 100,000 galaxies. It spans more than 500 million light-years across space and represents our true cosmic neighborhood.

The name "Laniakea" comes from the Hawaiian language and means "Immense Heaven" or "Boundless Sky", a fitting name for one of the largest known structures in the nearby universe.

🌌 Did You Know?

Before 2014, astronomers believed our galaxy belonged only to the Virgo Supercluster. Improved galaxy mapping revealed that the Virgo Supercluster is actually part of the much larger Laniakea Supercluster.

How Big is Laniakea?

Laniakea stretches across approximately 520 million light-years and contains a mass equivalent to around 100 quadrillion Suns. It includes nearly 100,000 galaxies grouped into numerous galaxy clusters and galaxy groups.

Where is the Milky Way?

Our Solar System lies within the Milky Way Galaxy. The Milky Way belongs to the Local Group, which is one of many galaxy groups inside the Laniakea Supercluster. This makes Laniakea our largest known "cosmic home."

The Great Attractor

One of the most fascinating features of Laniakea is the Great Attractor, a mysterious region with immense gravitational influence. Although hidden behind dense regions of the Milky Way, its gravity appears to pull thousands of galaxies—including our own—toward it.

How Was Laniakea Discovered?

In 2014, astronomers analyzed the motion of thousands of nearby galaxies. Instead of grouping galaxies by their positions alone, they mapped how galaxies flow through space under gravity. This revealed that many galaxy groups share a common gravitational destination, defining the boundaries of the Laniakea Supercluster.

Dark Matter and Gravity

Most of Laniakea's mass is invisible dark matter. Its gravity influences the movement of galaxy clusters across hundreds of millions of light-years, shaping the large-scale structure of the universe.

How Scientists Study Laniakea

Astronomers use observations from powerful telescopes and galaxy surveys to map the motions of galaxies throughout space. Instead of simply measuring where galaxies are located, scientists analyze how gravity influences their movement. This technique revealed that thousands of galaxies flow toward the same gravitational region, defining the boundaries of the Laniakea Supercluster.

The Cosmic Web

Laniakea is only one part of the vast Cosmic Web—an enormous network of galaxy clusters, superclusters, and filaments separated by gigantic empty regions called cosmic voids. This web-like structure extends across the observable universe and represents the largest known pattern in nature.

The Future of Laniakea

Over billions of years, gravity will continue to reshape the galaxies within Laniakea. Galaxy groups and clusters will slowly interact, merge, and evolve. While the expansion of the universe causes distant superclusters to move farther apart, galaxies inside gravitationally bound regions like Laniakea remain connected.

Why is Laniakea Important?

Understanding Laniakea helps astronomers determine our exact location in the universe and reveals how gravity organizes galaxies on enormous scales. It also provides important evidence for the existence of dark matter and helps scientists investigate the evolution of the cosmic web.
