🌌 The Local Group

Discover our cosmic neighborhood—a family of galaxies bound together by gravity across millions of light-years.

17 July 2026 11 min read Study Circle Explorer

What is the Local Group?

The Local Group is a collection of more than 100 galaxies held together by gravity. It spans roughly 10 million light-years and includes our own Milky Way, the Andromeda Galaxy (M31), the Triangulum Galaxy (M33), and dozens of smaller dwarf galaxies.

Although these galaxies are separated by enormous distances, gravity binds them into a single galactic family moving through space together.

🌌 Did You Know?

The Milky Way and Andromeda contain most of the Local Group's total mass.

The Largest Members

Three spiral galaxies dominate the Local Group:

  • 🌍 Milky Way – Our home galaxy.
  • 🌌 Andromeda Galaxy (M31) – The largest galaxy in the Local Group.
  • ✨ Triangulum Galaxy (M33) – The third-largest member.

Dwarf Galaxies

Surrounding the three large galaxies are dozens of dwarf galaxies. Some orbit the Milky Way, while others orbit Andromeda or Triangulum. These small galaxies contain millions to billions of stars and help astronomers understand how larger galaxies grow over time.

How Large is the Local Group?

The Local Group stretches across approximately 10 million light-years. Despite its enormous size, it is only a tiny part of the much larger Virgo Supercluster and the even greater Laniakea Supercluster.

How Do Galaxies Stay Together?

Gravity is the force that binds the Local Group. Massive halos of dark matter surrounding each galaxy contribute significantly to the group's overall gravitational pull, preventing the galaxies from drifting apart.

🚀 Quick Local Group Facts

  • ✔ More than 100 known galaxies
  • ✔ Diameter: About 10 million light-years
  • ✔ Dominated by the Milky Way and Andromeda
  • ✔ Includes the Triangulum Galaxy
  • ✔ Contains dozens of dwarf galaxies
  • ✔ Part of the Virgo Supercluster

Dark Matter in the Local Group

Scientists estimate that most of the Local Group's total mass consists of invisible dark matter. Its gravitational influence controls the motions of galaxies and helps hold the entire group together over billions of years.

The Local Group in Motion

The galaxies within the Local Group are constantly moving under the influence of gravity. The Milky Way and Andromeda are slowly approaching one another, while many dwarf galaxies orbit these larger systems. Over billions of years, gravitational interactions will reshape the entire Local Group.

The Future of the Local Group

Astronomers predict that the Milky Way and Andromeda will begin merging in about 4.5 billion years. This cosmic event will take billions more years to complete, eventually creating one massive galaxy. The Triangulum Galaxy (M33) may also become part of this merger in the distant future.

How Scientists Study the Local Group

Space telescopes such as the Hubble Space Telescope, the James Webb Space Telescope, and ESA's Gaia mission allow astronomers to measure the positions, motions, and compositions of millions of stars. These observations reveal how galaxies interact and evolve over cosmic time.

Why the Local Group Matters

The Local Group provides astronomers with a nearby laboratory for studying galaxy formation, dark matter, star formation, and gravitational interactions. Because its galaxies are relatively close, scientists can observe details that would be impossible to see in more distant galaxy groups.

🚀 Amazing Local Group Facts

  • ✔ Contains more than 100 known galaxies.
  • ✔ Spans roughly 10 million light-years.
  • ✔ The Milky Way and Andromeda dominate its total mass.
  • ✔ Most member galaxies are dwarf galaxies.
  • ✔ Dark matter makes up most of the group's mass.
  • ✔ It belongs to the larger Virgo Supercluster within the Laniakea Supercluster.

Beyond the Local Group

The Local Group is only a tiny part of the observable universe. It is one of thousands of galaxy groups that make up the Virgo Supercluster, which itself is part of the enormous Laniakea Supercluster. Beyond these structures lie billions of other galaxies spread across the observable universe.

🌌 Quick Summary

• The Local Group is a gravitationally bound collection of more than 100 galaxies.
• The Milky Way, Andromeda, and Triangulum are its three largest members.
• It spans about 10 million light-years.
• Dark matter holds the group together.
• The Milky Way and Andromeda are expected to merge in about 4.5 billion years.
